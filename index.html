Data Methodology

Federal Disaster Response:
Measuring Declaration Lag

An analysis of FEMA Major Disaster Declarations from the Eisenhower administration to the present, examining whether response times have changed over time.

Dataset FEMA Disaster Declarations Summaries v2 ↗ Source data
01 — Core Variable

Measuring Days to Declaration

The dataset spans the Eisenhower administration to the present, where each row represents a county-level disaster designation. The variable days_to_declaration measures the number of calendar days between incidentBeginDate and declarationDate using difftime() in R, restricted to Major Disaster Declarations only (declarationType == "DR") as these usually indicate more broad federal assistance.

02 — Data Cleaning

Grouping at the State Level

Because each row is a county designation rather than a unique disaster, the data needed to be cleaned. A check of ~3,000 state-incident combinations found only 13 with variation in declaration dates within a single state, which were nearly all attributable to tribal nations receiving separate, typically later, declarations. The analysis groups at the state level and takes the earliest declaration date within each state-incident group — tribal declarations tend to come later and are treated as a separate phenomenon. County-level rows are kept to report minimum, median, and maximum lag per state rather than reducing to a single number. Median is used as the primary statistic over mean to avoid a single outlier county disproportionately skewing state-wide data.

03 — Storm Selection

Helene and Comparable Storms

Hurricane Helene is isolated by incidentId rather than name string, as a string search for “helene” returns rows from an older unrelated storm since FEMA reuses storm names. Hurricanes Camille, Floyd, Frances, Ivan, Jeanne, Harvey, Ida, Matthew, and Idalia were selected as comparable storms because of their similar rapid intensification and extreme rainfall. For the comparable storms analysis, both a median and a simple unweighted mean were calculated — a weighted average by population or damage estimates would be more rigorous but would require joining an additional dataset. One NA that appeared in the unweighted mean chart turned out to be Hurricane Ian’s Seminole Tribe of Florida declaration, which shared an incidentId with the broader Ian declaration — it is kept labeled separately as “Ian - Seminole (2022)” in the comparable storms analysis.

04 — Tribal Declarations

Isolating Tribal Response Lag

For tribal declarations specifically, filtering used both the tribalRequest flag and a string search across designatedArea for keywords like “indian nation,” “indian reservation,” “pueblo,” and “tribe of.” Tribal and non-tribal declarations within the same state-incident were then separated to compare lag differences directly, pivoting the data wide to put tribal and non-tribal days side by side. Florida was also isolated specifically given it has a clear compare/contrast case within the same state.

05 — Trend Analysis

Patterns Over Time

The overall trend over time was examined for all hurricane and tropical storm DR declarations, plotting days_to_declaration against incidentBeginDate with a smoothed trend line. This was then broken down further by decade and by presidential administration using both mean and median.

Limitations
Governor request requirement. The lag clock starts at incident begin, but FEMA cannot declare until a state governor formally requests it, meaning some delay may reflect state-level decisions rather than federal response time.
Pre-NEMIS data entry. Data manually entered before the 1998 launch of the National Emergency Management Information System may be subject to a small percentage of human error.
